Introduction
Electronic textiles are a major focus of recent efforts in
material science and textile engineering, with poten-
tial applications ranging from health care (Bandodkar
et al. 2016; Chen et al. 2015; Gao et al. 2016;
Majumder et al. 2017; Rim et al. 2016; Xiao et al.
2018a, b) to communication (Hussain et al. 2015) and
energy storage (Fan et al. 2016; Liu et al. 2017a, b; Wu
et al. 2016). In the context of the progress of internet
technology and miniaturization of electronic devices,
electronic textiles are considered as promising and
achievable targets for next generation electronics
(Kim et al. 2017; Zeng et al. 2014; Ghosh et al.
2018a, b). A critical component in the electronic
textiles is electrically conductive fabrics because they
not only act as fundamental conductors to connect
other parts like batteries, energy storage devices, and
sensors, but also be incorporated into textiles by
simple textile manufacturing technologies like tailor-
ing or sewing (Wang et al. 2017). Besides, conductive
fabrics themselves are a class of functional materials
for various applications, especially in electric heating,
electrostatic
dissipation,
electromagnetic
wave
absorption, and medical electrodes (Liu et al. 2016;
Yetisen et al. 2016; Ghosh et al. 2018a, b; Mondal
et al. 2017). After years of experimental and theoret-
ical research, conductive fabric is now reasonably
developed, and a number of fabricating strategies of
conductive fabric have been reported. Technologies
like spinning yarns by blending metal ï¬bers or
embroidering conductive yarns to common fabrics
have been the primary methodologies to fabricate
conductive fabrics (Ghahremani Honarvar and Latiï¬?
2016; Lai et al. 2017; Stoppa and Chiolerio 2014; Xiao
et al. 2018a, b). Unfortunately, these techniques
encountered a series of practical problems such as
insufï¬cient wearing comfortability, inï¬‚exibility and
unstable conductivity against deformation. More
recently, various coating strategies including printing
(Guo et al. 2016; Saleem and Leandro 2017), surface
micro-dissolution (Li et al. 2016), dipping and drying
(Doga et al. 2016), and deposition (Cheng et al. 2017;
Huang et al. 2016; Zhang et al. 2017a, b) have been
investigated using conjugate polymer (Hebeish et al.
2016; Liu et al. 2016; Lu et al. 2016), metal (Cheng
et al. 2015; Ma et al. 2015, Nechyporchuk et al. 2017;
Zhu et al. 2017; He et al. 2018), and carbon nano-
material (Cataldi et al. 2017; Di et al. 2016; Hu et al.
2010; Jeon et al. 2017; Molina 2016; Zhang et al.
2017a, b) as the conductive coating material. Although
these approaches have allowed conductive fabrics to
be obtained with improved ï¬‚exibility and stretchabil-
ity, few methods can provide modiï¬ed fabrics with
durable conductivity and approving fatigue resistance
(Zeng et al. 2014). The main reason is the ï¬‚aking off of
the conducting layer caused by large and frequent
deformation. Thus, fabrication of conductive fabrics
having high fatigue durability via a scalable and cost-
effective process remain as substantial challenges.
In the present work, a unique design of a conductive
coating over cotton fabric substrate to improve the
electrical stability is proposed. The coating has a
hierarchical structure composed of an interlayer of
silver nanoparticles (Ag NPs), a top layer of conduc-
tive hydrogel, and a bottom layer of L-cysteine binder
(Fig. 1a). The L-cysteine layer is designed to enhance
adsorptive interaction between the Ag NPs and the
surface of cotton ï¬bers. Previous work (Kwak et al.

linked on the cotton ï¬ber surface are very effective for

afï¬nity of the thiol groups to the Ag NPs. The top layer

We synthesize a poly (acrylic acid) based hydrogel

soft, electrically stable, and durable against deforma-
tion. This new coating strategy has potential as a
scalable method to impact textile with practical
conductive function.
